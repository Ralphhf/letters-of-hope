On the stigma

Writing a prisoner isn't condoning a crime

People sometimes hesitate to write, afraid of what it means or who might judge them. Let's name it plainly: offering someone dignity is not excusing their past. It's affirming that they still have a future — and a soul. You're not signing off on anything. You're simply refusing to let another human being vanish.

How to write

Your first letter, step by step

No experience needed. A short, sincere note means more than a perfect one.

01

Keep it simple & warm

Introduce yourself by first name, say why you're writing, and share something ordinary about your day. Avoid asking about their case unless they bring it up.

02

Address it exactly

Copy the name, ID number, and facility address precisely — the ID number is required, or mail won't be delivered. Include a return address (a P.O. box is fine).

03

Follow the mail rules

Most facilities accept plain letters and photos, but ban stickers, glitter, cash, and anything laminated. When unsure, keep it to paper and pen. See our FAQ for details.

04

Be patient & consistent

Replies can take weeks and mail is screened. Consistency is the gift — even a note every few weeks tells someone they're not forgotten.
