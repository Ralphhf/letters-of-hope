The letter that started it all

Sample post — this is placeholder writing to show how an article looks. You'll replace it with your own.

The first letter I ever sent to a prison came back unopened. I'd forgotten the ID number, and without it, the mailroom had no way to know who it was for. That small failure taught me the first lesson of this work: the details matter, because the person on the other end matters.

The second letter got through. Three weeks later, a reply arrived in careful handwriting. It didn't say much — a thank you, a few lines about the view from a barred window, a question about the world outside. But it ended with a sentence I've never forgotten.

“I had started to think I was already dead to everyone. Your letter proved me wrong.”

That was the beginning. Not a plan, not an organization — just the realization that a stamp and ten minutes could tell someone they still existed in the eyes of another human being.

Why letters, and why now

There are people serving long sentences who will go years without mail. Families drift, friends move on, and the silence becomes its own punishment. Into that silence, a letter lands like light through a slot in the door.

We're Catholic in our roots, and the call is old and clear: visit the imprisoned. Most of us can't visit. But almost all of us can write. That's the whole idea behind this ministry — to turn a corporal work of mercy into something anyone can do from their kitchen table.

If you've read this far, you're probably the kind of person who could write one too. When you're ready, we'll help you take the first step.
